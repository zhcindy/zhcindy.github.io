Injection Molded Toy Design

A 4-person, 10-week project that injection molded a
4th grader's idea of a rocketship toy.

    My Role

  • Modeled (using NX/UG) majority of part and mold
  • Contributed to conceptual design
  • Contributed to CNC programming

The Starting Point: A 4th Grade Sketch

Our inspiration came from this sketch by a student at a local elementary school. The 4th grade class was involved in a tech outreach program in connection with Northwestern University. After learning how toys are made, the students were told to design their own, with the potential for it to be brought to life.

Part Design: Identical Halves + Nose Cone

For a simpler design, our rocketship body was composed of 2 identical halves secured together with a nose cone. (Sketches by a fellow team member).

Rocketship fuselage design, 2 identical halves would snap together.

Nose cone design, initially would be two halves (right) but later changed to one single part (left).

Modeling: Part and Mold

I modeled majority of the part and mold for the project, using Siemens NX (UG). We considered factors such as interferance fits and moldability (on the part), and material flow, shrinkage, and gating locations (on the mold).

Machining and Injection Molding

Both cavity and core molds were programmed using NX to be machined by a Haas CNC machine. The molds were post-processed for tool marks and loaded up into the injection molding machine for a small production run of 75 parts. Each cycle produced one full assembly.

Molded Parts and Takeaways

Multiple common injection molding issues occurred, such as flash and bowing. The injection molding parameters such as cycle time and injection pressure were adjusted to yield the best possible result. One of the main improvements for future iterations would be to decrease the part thickness for better material flow and to address the bowing issue.
